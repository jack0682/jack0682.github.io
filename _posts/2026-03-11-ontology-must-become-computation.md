Today I wanted to write down a set of propositions that kept returning while I was coding.  
This is not an ONN Daily entry, not a theorem note, and not a polished paper section.  
It is a personal record of the strict direction I believe the work must take.

## Twenty Propositions

1. Any concept named in computer science must admit a computable form.
2. An unformalized concept does not yet exist in the machine world.
3. Ontology must be a reasoning substrate, not merely a naming layer.
4. To claim ontology is to commit it to computation.
5. Topology is mathematics only when it is rendered as an operational structure.
6. Topological intuition is not equivalent to topological computation.
7. A genuinely new predictive model must be judged by a new principle of reasoning, not by architectural packaging.
8. An LLM-centered system may be ontology-augmented, but it is not ontology-native.
9. A truly new direction begins when relations constrain action rather than merely support memory.
10. The world may be formed from a relational field prior to the emergence of discrete entities.
11. A world description object must be a computational object, not a natural-language summary.
12. ONN must function as a constraint-based convergence operator for reasoning, not as a labeling mechanism.
13. ORTSF must realize reasoning traces in time-sensitive control.
14. The core research chain is: Raw world -> RelationsWorld world formation -> ONN reasoning -> ORTSF control.
15. Formalization must incorporate exceptional cases into the rule system rather than leave them implicit.
16. Strictness is not a personal preference but an ontological demand of the binary machine world.
17. A researcher must not merely invoke grand concepts but assign them computational status.
18. My research is directed toward building a machine that computes existence and relations rather than merely describing them.
19. Ontology as wording is insufficient; ontology must become computation.
20. Topology as metaphor is insufficient; topology must become operator.

I wrote these down because I do not want to hide behind language that sounds large but computes nothing.  
If a concept is real in machine research, it must survive formalization, enter the reasoning chain, and constrain action.

This is the direction I am thinking through now: not a machine that only describes the world, but a machine that computes relations, forms a world, reasons over it, and acts under constraint.
