A long book-in-progress (working title: *RelationWorld Theory*) that
develops the mathematical machinery the rest of the programme depends
on. The object of study is a **weighted graph equipped with a discrete
gauge structure** — every edge carries both a positive weight and a
group-valued transition. The question is: *what can be said about
this object with the tools of algebraic topology and gauge theory?*

The answer turns out to be: quite a lot. Yang–Mills gauge theory,
Uhlenbeck compactness, and Cheeger spectral geometry all have clean
combinatorial analogues in this setting, and together they give a
picture where natural aggregates (*fruits*), internal singularities
(*doors*), and existence itself become gauge-invariant topological
invariants computable by finite linear algebra.

## The six axioms

- **A0.** Structure group $G$ is a compact Lie group with bi-invariant metric.
- **A1.** Finite node set $V$, $|V| = n$.
- **A2.** Discrete time set $\mathbb{T} \subseteq \mathbb{Z}$.
- **A3.** Fruit threshold $\theta \in (0, 1)$.
- **A4.** Door threshold $\tau > 0$.
- **A5.** Intrinsic data axiom — boundary coupling is internally computable.

## The eight main theorems

<Theorem kind="theorem" number="A" name="Energy Isolation">
Fruit internal energy is at least $1 - \theta$ of the total.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="theorem" number="B" name="Finiteness of Doors">
$|\Sigma| \le E_{\mathrm{total}} / \tau$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="theorem" number="C" name="Self-Interpretation">
The interior determines its own door set.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="theorem" number="D" name="Metastability">
Escape time is bounded below by $1 / (2\theta)$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="theorem" number="E" name="Curvature Localisation">
Curvature decays exponentially from doors.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="theorem" number="F" name="Spectral Stability">
Small weight perturbation implies small conductance change.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="theorem" number="G" name="Door Stability">
Small weight perturbation implies stable door set.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="theorem" number="H" name="Flow Stability">
The Yang–Mills gradient flow converges to a stable equilibrium.
</Theorem>

Full proofs live in [Part II of the notes](/notes/).
