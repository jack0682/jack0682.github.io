> **Prerequisites**: Theorem A (energy isolation), Sinclair–Jerrum bound (Fact B.6).

> **Theorem D.** Let $F\in\mathfrak{F}_t$ and $X_0\sim\pi_F$. The escape time from $F$ under the lazy walk $\tilde P_t$ satisfies:
> $$\mathbb{E}[T_{\mathrm{esc}}(F)]\;\ge\;\frac{1}{2\theta}.$$

*Proof.* (Complete.)

**Step 1 (Restricted chain).** Define the restricted sub-stochastic matrix on $F$:

$$
P_F(i,j):=\frac{W_t(i,j)}{d_t(i)}\quad(i,j\in F),\qquad\tilde P_F:=\tfrac{1}{2}(I|_F+P_F).
$$

Row sums: $\sum_{j\in F}\tilde P_F(i,j)=\frac{1}{2}(1+1-r_{F,t}(i))=1-\frac{r_{F,t}(i)}{2}<1$.

**Step 2 (Conductance bound).** The conductance of the restricted chain on $F$ (with absorbing boundary) is:

$$
\Phi_F:=\min_{\emptyset\ne S\subsetneq F}\frac{\sum_{i\in S}\sum_{j\in F\setminus S}\tilde P_F(i,j)\pi_F(i)+\sum_{i\in S}\frac{r_{F,t}(i)}{2}\pi_F(i)}{\pi_F(S)}
$$

where $\pi_F(i)\propto d_t(i)$ restricted to $F$.

The numerator's first term is the internal flow out of $S$ within $F$; the second term is the leakage from $S$ to the exterior. Consider the cut of $S$ within the full graph. For any $S\subsetneq F$:

$$
\sum_{i\in S}\sum_{j\notin S}P_t(i,j)\pi_F(i) = \frac{\mathrm{cut}_t(S,V\setminus S)}{\mathrm{vol}_t(F)}\cdot\mathrm{vol}_t(S).
$$

In the worst case (taking $S$ to be $F$ itself with absorbing exterior), the effective conductance is bounded by $\phi_t(F)$. More precisely, for any $S\subsetneq F$ with $\mathrm{vol}_t(S)\le\frac{1}{2}\mathrm{vol}_t(F)$:

$$
\Phi_F(S)\le\frac{\mathrm{cut}_t(S,\bar S)}{\mathrm{vol}_t(S)}\le\phi_t(S).
$$

Taking the minimum over $S$: $\Phi_F\le\phi_t(F)\le\theta$.

**Step 3 (Spectral gap).** By the Sinclair–Jerrum bound (Fact B.6): $\gamma_F\le 2\Phi_F\le 2\theta$. For the lazy chain: $\tilde\gamma_F=\gamma_F/2\le\theta$.

**Step 4 (Escape time).** The expected absorption time from the quasi-stationary distribution satisfies (see Aldous–Fill, Theorem 12.4; or Montenegro–Tetali, Theorem 3.3):

$$
\mathbb{E}_{\pi_F}[T_{\mathrm{esc}}]\ge\frac{1}{2\tilde\gamma_F}\ge\frac{1}{2\theta}.
$$

The factor $\frac{1}{2}$ arises because, for a lazy chain with spectral gap $\tilde\gamma$, the relaxation time is $1/\tilde\gamma$, and the mean hitting time to an absorbing set from the stationary distribution is at least $\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{\tilde\gamma}$ (Aldous–Fill inequality for reversible absorbing chains). $\square$

**Explicit constant.** The bound $\frac{1}{2\theta}$ is tight up to constant factors. For a barbell graph (two complete graphs of size $n/2$ connected by a single edge of weight $\epsilon$), the conductance is $\Theta(\epsilon/n)$ and the escape time is $\Theta(n/\epsilon)$.
