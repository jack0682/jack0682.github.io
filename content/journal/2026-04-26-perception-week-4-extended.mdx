- **F-1, M-1, MO-1 — all resolved (2026-04-24).** The three Critical open problems that had been blocking the v2.0 release for nearly a year all closed in a single day. SPLIT-RESOLVED / LAYER-CLARIFIED / SIDESTEPPED, respectively. The lesson was unexpected: none of them was really an open question — each was a *framing error* in different disguise.

## The 8-day arc — what happened, day by day

Eight days total (one extended). The arc starts in confusion, passes through architecture-building, lands an empirical pivot, and ends with verification that catches its own bug.

**Day 1 — Sun, 2026-04-19. The N-1 reframing.** A year of work on F-1 / M-1 / MO-1 was getting nowhere because it kept asking the same question: "How can K&gt;1 be energetically stable when isoperimetric ordering says K=1 is cheaper?" The first move of W4 was to refuse that vocabulary entirely. Five orthogonal probes — axiom stress, hidden-assumption excavation, top-down ontology critique, external-framework comparison, bottom-up rebuild — produced nine new candidate problem-statements (P-A through P-H). The single source underneath all of them turned out to be **N-1 (Soft–Hard Switching Asymmetry)**: the theory's ontology is continuous (a graded soft field) but the operational level switches to discrete entities (integer K, threshold rules, axiom branching). F, M, and MO are three faces of N-1.

**Day 2 — Mon, 2026-04-20. Candidate evaluation.** Eight candidate frameworks (A through E plus combinations) were evaluated on two matrices: which open problems each candidate fully resolves (×16 OPs), and which Cat A theorems survive each candidate (×49 theorems). After three audit rounds, the Pareto frontier came out as B, B+C, E, and C+E — with E (Emergent-K) and C+E (kinetic + emergent) leading on coverage. This day was mostly bookkeeping — but it cleared the deck for the day-3 architecture decision.

**Day 3 — Tue, 2026-04-21. K_soft + thermal framework.** A 14-round verification session settled the architectural choice: replace the implicit hard-K commitment with a *soft* K-counter $K_{\mathrm{soft}}$ (persistence-based, real-valued) and reformulate the energy as a thermal free-energy $\mathcal{F}_{C+E}[u] = \mathcal{E} - TS + \lambda_K \cdot K_{\mathrm{soft}}$. The three Critical OPs visibly *moved* under this reframing — they no longer pointed to a missing mechanism, but to a structural choice that could be made differently.

**Day 4 — Wed, 2026-04-22. Symmetry / moduli (SF) deepening.** A 24-round session on the symmetry side: $\Phi_4$ on non-D₄ graphs, continuous automorphism groups + Morse-Bott, Proposition 1.3b (d) full spectrum, universal $c_0$, tree-saturation. Most of these are technical infill, but cumulatively they made the day-6 σ-framework possible.

**Day 5 — Thu, 2026-04-23. Stage 2 axiom audit + Orbital empirical pivot.** Five Cat A results from Tier 1, including the **R23 Orbital Discovery**: 56 stable minimizers on the test grid, each cleanly classified by orbital type — and the closure operator visibly *removes* F=1 disks in this regime. The empirical evidence finally lined up with the W4 reframing: the F=1 single-disk wasn't even surviving as an attractor in practice.

**Day 6 — Fri, 2026-04-24. The peak.** A 28-file day. **σ-framework** introduced (definitional Cat A). **Theorem 2 family** proved (Cat A graph-class independent via Theorem 2-G). **V5b** initial form. **F-1, M-1, MO-1 all resolved** — see the dedicated section below. **CN15 Static / Dynamic Separation** stated explicitly. The day that made W4 a Critical-resolution week.

**Day 7 — Sat, 2026-04-25. Verify and close.** D₄ irreducible-representation classification (G3). σ-framework numerical verification (G2 — three NQs). NQ-168 commensurability splitting (G1). Weekly summary written. CV-1.3 prepared and merged.

**Day 8 (extended) — Sun, 2026-04-26. The σ-framework also strengthened from single-graph empirical to multi-graph empirical Cat A. CV-1.4 merged the same evening.

The shape of the week, in one line: **a problem that had eight months of dead ends turned out to be a misunderstanding of which layer the question lived on**.

## What's new — four new Category A entries

These four theorems together accomplish something the rest of the canonical had been edging toward without quite reaching: they make SCC's *pre-objective character* — the claim that objects are derivative and the cohesion field is primitive — into a proved theorem rather than a design intent.

### W4 close (2026-04-24): the Pre-Objective Mechanism cluster

| T-ID | Statement (summary) | Source |
|------|---------------------|--------|
| **T-PreObj-1** | Under full SCC parameters, the F=1 single-disk minimizer is **not a critical point**. Gradient flow attracts to multi-peak F≥2 configurations. IC-protocol dichotomy: adaptive bounded protocols stay bounded; random initialization scales as $\sim L^{2.8}$. | C-0700 / P-0700 |
| **T-PreObj-1G** | Conclusions (i),(ii) of T-PreObj-1 hold on **any** finite connected graph satisfying (G1)–(G4). Graph-class independent. | C-0701 / P-0701 |
| **Lemma 4** | The inner-product matrix $M \in \mathbb{R}^{2 \times 2}$ of the gradients $g_{cl}, g_{sep}$ is positive definite under linear independence. Destabilization magnitude $\Lambda^T M \Lambda > 0$ for any $\Lambda \neq 0$. | C-0702 / P-0702 |
| **F-1 Resolution Corollary** | F-1 splits into two layers. Pure $\mathcal{E}_{bd}$ portion: T-Merge (b) (pre-existing canonical). Full SCC portion: T-PreObj-1 (i). Both Category A. | (corollary) |

**What T-PreObj-1 actually says, in plain language.** Imagine a single round disk of cohesion sitting on a graph — the simplest "F=1" formation, like one isolated object. On the pure boundary-morphology energy $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{bd}}$ (closure and separation turned off), this disk is a critical point; perturbing it costs energy. Now turn closure and separation back on — that is, run the *full* SCC energy. The theorem says: this same disk is *no longer* a critical point. Gradient flow doesn't sit at it; gradient flow leaves it. And it leaves it for a multi-peak configuration with two or more separated formations. So the natural attractor of full SCC isn't a single object — it's multiple objects.

This is exactly what the theory had been *claiming* about pre-objective character all along. T-PreObj-1 makes the claim into a theorem.

**What Lemma 4 contributes to the proof.** Lemma 4 is the technical heart. At the F=1 disk, the gradient of $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{bd}}$ vanishes (it's a critical point of *that* energy), but the gradients of the closure energy $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{cl}}$ and the separation energy $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{sep}}$ generically do not. Lemma 4 shows that these two non-vanishing gradients are linearly independent in a strong sense: the 2×2 matrix of their inner products is positive definite. That positive definiteness is what guarantees the F=1 configuration *cannot* be a critical point of full SCC, regardless of the relative weights $\lambda_{\mathrm{cl}}$ and $\lambda_{\mathrm{sep}}$. It's a small lemma with a load-bearing consequence.

**What T-PreObj-1G adds.** The original T-PreObj-1 was proved on the test grid graphs. T-PreObj-1G shows that the proof never used grid-specific features — only abstract spectral and isoperimetric properties guaranteed by the (G1)–(G4) hypotheses (connectedness, regularity, finite diameter, mild spectral gap). So the conclusion lifts from "true on these grids" to "true on **any** finite connected graph in this class." That's why the pre-objective character of SCC is not an artifact of the substrate.

**What the F-1 Resolution Corollary does.** F-1 was originally framed as "K=1 is cheaper than K=2 (T-Merge (b)) but we observe K&gt;1 — paradox." The Corollary makes this precise by splitting it: the K=1-cheaper claim is correct on pure $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{bd}}$ (already canonical as T-Merge (b)), and the K&gt;1 observation is correct on full SCC (newly canonical as T-PreObj-1). The two claims live on different energies. They never disagreed; the apparent paradox was a category error.

**Verification**: E-0090 (numerical study at L=12, three-digit theory–experiment agreement on the destabilization magnitudes); E-0091 (L=32 study of the IC-protocol dichotomy).

### W4-extended close (2026-04-26): the Pre-Objective Goldstone

T-PreObj-1 establishes *that* F≥2 is the attractor. T-V5b-T then asks: when symmetry is broken by selecting a particular multi-peak configuration, what *kind* of soft modes (zero-frequency excitations) does the broken symmetry generate?

| T-ID | Statement (summary) | Source |
|------|---------------------|--------|
| **T-V5b-T** | On translation-invariant graphs (torus $T^d$, cycle $C_n$): a sub/super-lattice spectral dichotomy at the F=1 / F≥2 transition. In 2D, Goldstone modes form a **2-fold doublet with commensurability splitting**. In 1D, a **1-fold Goldstone branch**. **Goldstone nodal count = 2 universal**. After 8 V5b iterations (V1 → V5b''). | C-0710 / P-0710 |

**What T-V5b-T says, in plain language.** When you pick a specific multi-peak formation, you've broken the translation symmetry of the underlying graph (the formation now has a particular position rather than being symmetric under shifts). Whenever a continuous symmetry is broken, the theory of Goldstone bosons says you get zero-frequency modes — one per broken generator of the symmetry group. T-V5b-T applies this to SCC on translation-invariant graphs:

- On a 2D torus, translation has 2 generators (x and y), so there should be 2 Goldstone modes — and there are. They form a *doublet*. When the formation period and the lattice period are commensurate, the doublet is degenerate (the two modes have exactly the same frequency); incommensurate, they split.
- On a 1D cycle, translation has 1 generator, so there should be 1 Goldstone mode — and there is. Single branch.
- Across all tested cases — 2D torus periodic, 2D torus free boundary, 1D cycle — the Goldstone modes have *exactly two nodes per period*. Universal, regardless of dimension.

The universal nodal count is the strongest part: it's a topological feature of the broken-symmetry signature on SCC, independent of graph class.

**Why eight iterations were needed.** The first version V1 was too narrow (specific to one graph). V2 generalized but missed the commensurability structure. V3, V4 had bugs. V5a partially worked but had a subtle indexing problem in the analysis script — the eighth iteration (V5b'') re-verified using a mode-agnostic detection method and finally settled the result. The full story is in the Reproducibility section.

**Verification**: E-0095 ($\zeta$-scan), E-0096 (graph-class extension + nodal count), E-0097 (mode-agnostic re-check after NQ-172).

## What's clarified / resolved — all three Critical entries closed

The single biggest result of W4. For nearly a year, every theorem touching multi-formation came with a footnote: "blocked by F-1 / M-1 / MO-1 until we resolve those." All three are now closed. Here is what each one was, why it had been blocking everything, and what the resolution actually is.

### F-1 (K=2 Vacuity) — ✅ SPLIT-RESOLVED

**What F-1 was.** The K=2 theorems (two formations existing simultaneously) were derived under an assumption that each formation's mass $m_j$ was held fixed externally. If you remove that assumption and allow $m_j$ to vary freely, energy minimization always prefers $m_j \to 0$ — the smaller formation gets squeezed to zero, and you collapse back to K=1. So K=2 was "vacuous" without external scaffolding.

**Why this was a Critical blocker.** F-1 effectively meant the K-field theory was not self-contained — it required someone outside the theory to keep telling it "K=2, masses fixed." That undermined the theory's claim to explain how multi-formation arises.

**The resolution (2026-04-24).** F-1 splits into two layers, each of which turns out to be a *Category A theorem* rather than an open problem.

- **Pure $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{bd}}$ portion.** On the boundary-morphology energy alone, "K=1 has lower energy than K=2" really is true. This is the isoperimetric ordering on connected graphs, already canonical as **T-Merge (b)**. It was always a theorem; calling it "F-1" had it shelved under the wrong filename.
- **Full SCC portion.** Under full SCC parameters, the F=1 single-disk minimizer of pure $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{bd}}$ is **not a critical point** (T-PreObj-1 (i)). So the comparison "K=1 minimum vs K=2 minimum" doesn't even survive into the full SCC landscape — F=1 is non-critical, F≥2 is the default.

**Net effect**: the comparison that looked like a paradox ("K=1 cheaper static minimum vs empirically observed multi-formation") simply dissolves. A pre-existing Cat A theorem plus a new Cat A theorem together remove the dichotomy's premise. F-1 wasn't a problem to be solved; it was a misframing to be exposed.

### M-1 (K=1 Energetic Preference) — ✅ LAYER-CLARIFIED

**What M-1 was.** A direct calculation showed that the K=2 energy landscape on $(m_1, m_2)$ with $m_1 + m_2 = M$ is monotonically decreasing as one of the formations shrinks. So K=1 with the entire mass $M$ is *always* energetically cheaper than any K=2 split. This was the root cause of F-1: even with masses constrained, energy still wants K=1.

**Why this was a Critical blocker.** Together with F-1, M-1 said: SCC has no internal preference for K&gt;1, ever. Any observed multi-formation has to be either externally imposed (F-1) or kinetically trapped (which is what the kinetic-multi-formation literature had assumed). The theory had no in-built mechanism for genuine multi-formation.

**The resolution (2026-04-24).** M-1 is **not an open problem at all**. It is the *correct mathematical statement* (T-Merge (b)) — isoperimetric ordering on the constraint manifold $\Sigma_m$. The original framing conflated two distinct quantities:

- **Pure $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{bd}}$ layer.** M-1 holds — K=1 has lower energy than K=2 through perimeter minimization. Already canonical as T-Merge (b).
- **Full SCC layer.** The comparison itself is not framed. Because the F=1 single-disk is non-critical (T-PreObj-1), the "K=1 ground state" of pure $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{bd}}$ does not survive into the full SCC landscape. The static minimum and the dynamic attractor refer to different objects.

**The conceptual key**: **CN15 Static / Dynamic Separation**. The static global minimum on pure $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{bd}}$ and the dynamic protocol-endpoint observables ($\widehat{K}, \mathcal{F}$) on full SCC live on different layers and need not match. Once that is stated explicitly, M-1's apparent contradiction with empirical observation dissolves: nothing said the static minimum has to equal the dynamic endpoint.

### MO-1 (Morse Theory Inapplicability) — ⚪ SIDESTEPPED

**What MO-1 was.** The constrained manifold $\Sigma^2_M = \{(u^1, u^2) : m_1 + m_2 = M\}$ for K=2 is *not* a smooth manifold — it has corners where one of the formations' mass goes to zero. Smooth Morse theory requires manifolds without boundary and is therefore not directly applicable. Several theorems (T-8-Core, T-14, parts of the persistence machinery) had been stated under the implicit assumption of a smooth manifold, raising the question whether they survived the corner structure.

**Why this was a Critical blocker.** It cast doubt on global landscape analysis on $\Sigma^K_M$ for any $K \geq 2$. Either we needed stratified Morse theory (a much more complex framework), or the smooth-Morse statements had to be downgraded.

**The resolution (2026-04-24).** SIDESTEPPED — for now. The W4 work introduced the **σ-framework**, a Hessian-eigenvalue / irrep / nodal-count signature
$$\sigma(u^*) = (\mathcal{F}; \{(n_k, [\rho_k], \lambda_k)\})$$
that operates on the **smooth single-formation manifold $\Sigma_m$ (no corners)**. The Theorem 2 family (Cat A, graph-class independent) also operates on the single-formation $\Sigma_m$. The principal W4 results therefore **do not require** Morse analysis on $\Sigma^K_M$. MO-1 is not a blocker for the current scope.

**Caveat**: this is genuinely a *sidestep*, not a resolution. Multi-formation extension (stratified Morse on $\Sigma^K_M$, multi-formation σ Phase 5) is genuine open work for W5+. MO-1 will become an active blocker again at that stage.

### Resolution path: Option D (premise dissolution)

When the F-1/M-1/MO-1 cluster was set up at the start of W4, three options were on the table:

- **Option A** — accept "fixed K is an external constraint" and stop trying to derive K&gt;1 from inside. Abandoned: it makes the theory incomplete.
- **Option B** — invent a K-selection mechanism (information criteria like BIC, free energy, or birth-death dynamics). This was the most-explored path historically; never converged.
- **Option C** — reformulate the theory in kinetic / metastability terms (K&gt;1 as long-lived but not globally optimal). Explored in the previous-year audit; partially successful but didn't close F-1.

**Option D, discovered in W4 — premise dissolution.** A fourth path that is none of the three above. The discovery is: F-1 / M-1 weren't asking *the right question*. They were asking how K=1 (the static minimum) could be reconciled with K&gt;1 (the dynamic observation), but those two quantities live on different layers. Once you state the layer separation explicitly (CN15), the original question stops needing an answer. The candidate-evaluation matrix from day 2 had been searching for an Option B/C answer and finding none — turns out the right path was outside the matrix.

This is a kind of resolution that is hard to plan for: it doesn't show up as a candidate in any structured search because it asks us to *reframe what the question even means*. The Pareto matrix work wasn't wasted, though — without going through it, we wouldn't have seen that the matrix itself was answering a wrong question, and Option D wouldn't have emerged.

## What was tried and didn't work

The point of recording negative results is honesty. Here are this week's:

- **V4 / V5a were retracted in-session twice** during the V5b iteration cycle. V4 had a sign error in the symmetry counting. V5a was correct but had the mode-indexing bug that NQ-172 later exposed. Healthy iterative process — the bug was caught before the result reached canonical.

- **The candidate evaluation matrix was answering the wrong question.** Day 2's effort produced a Pareto frontier — B, B+C, E, C+E — and a clean decision tree. The actual route taken (Option D, premise dissolution) was not in the candidate space. The matrix wasn't useless — it forced the kind of close reading that exposed the framing assumption — but its direct output was discarded. This is a recurring pattern in this kind of work: structured search is good for finding the question, less good for finding the answer.

- **P-Unified-1 was re-falsified.** The hypothesis "Persist degradation is monotone in $\Lambda_{\mathrm{coupling}}$" had been falsified earlier by exp49–50, but it kept reappearing as an implicit assumption in derivations. This week's σ-framework work re-confirmed the falsification: $\Lambda_{\mathrm{coupling}}$ is a *structural* classifier (it tells you whether two formations are well-separated, weakly interacting, or strongly interacting), but it is not a *dynamical* predictor (it doesn't determine the rate at which Persist degrades). This is now stated more sharply with the σ-framework's distinction between signature (structural) and trajectory (dynamical).

## Reproducibility / errata

### NQ-172 — Mode-indexing artifact in the Goldstone analysis script (identified + resolved 2026-04-26)

The most important process-side learning of the W4-extended close. This is the sort of thing every numerical research project encounters and most don't catch in time.

**Discovery.** During the eighth V5b iteration, while running a $\zeta$-scan on the 2D torus to nail down the commensurability splitting, the result looked subtly off — the splitting magnitude wasn't following the expected scaling. Tracing back through the analysis script revealed that **spectral modes were being indexed inconsistently**: when the eigenvalues were computed, the mode labels were assigned by sort order, which meant that at parameter values where two modes were nearly degenerate, the labels could swap silently. The numerical result was correct in raw terms, but the *interpretation* — which mode was "the" Goldstone mode — was unstable.

**Why this matters.** If V5b had been promoted to canonical at V1, V2, or V5a (any of the iterations that occurred *before* the artifact was caught), the resulting "theorem" would have been **right in its quantitative output but wrong in its interpretation**. The kind of error that's hard to detect in review because the numbers all check out.

**The fix.** V5b'' was re-verified using **mode-agnostic detection** — instead of identifying "the Goldstone mode" by label and then measuring its splitting, the new approach measures the dimension of the low-frequency subspace directly and counts nodes of basis-independent invariants. This is robust to mode reordering by construction. NQ-170c (graph-class extension + nodal count) provides the documented safeguard.

**Result: robustness on both sides preserved.**
- **Positive — T-V5b-T (Cat A canonical-ready)**: sub/super-lattice spectral dichotomy + 2D commensurability splitting + 1D Goldstone + universal nodal count = 2 — confirmed mode-agnostically.
- **Negative — V5b-F (Cat C, new finding)**: partial Goldstone on boundary-modified graphs. The boundary breaks translation invariance, so the Goldstone branches partially gap. Carried as NQ-173 to W5+ for full characterization.

**Significance**: catching the artifact after eight iterations is evidence that the verification process is healthy. The cost of doing a thorough $\zeta$-scan that exposed the bug was one extra day; the cost of having the bug ship to canonical would have been much higher. The general lesson: **verification is not a chore; it is a discovery channel**. NQ-172 was caught only because someone insisted on rerunning V5b properly even after V5a "looked fine."

## Process notes

### What the Pre-Objective Mechanism actually means for the theory

The combination of T-PreObj-1 and T-PreObj-1G is the W4 conceptual capstone. SCC has, for over a year, *claimed* a pre-objective character — "objects are derivative; the graded cohesion field is primitive." Before W4, this was a design intent expressed through axiom choices and ontological commitments, but it was not *forced* by any theorem. T-PreObj-1 makes it forced: under full SCC parameters, the F=1 single-disk (the closest analogue to "a single object") is *not even a critical point*, while F≥2 multi-peak configurations are the default attractor. **Single-object thinking is energetically inconsistent with the full SCC framework.**

T-PreObj-1G upgrades this from "true on grids" to "true on any finite connected graph satisfying (G1)–(G4)." The pre-objective character is therefore **graph-class independent** — a property of the SCC framework itself, not of any particular spatial substrate. This is the difference between a mathematical theory and a phenomenological model: the theory's claim is robust to changes in the substrate, the phenomenology's claim is calibrated to a particular setup.

### Static / Dynamic Separation (CN15) as the conceptual key

The deepest lesson of W4 is that the F-1 / M-1 paradox was never a missing mechanism — it was a missing *commitment*. The two quantities being conflated were:

- **Static**: the global minimum on pure $\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{bd}}$. Definite, computable, K=1 by T-Merge (b).
- **Dynamic**: the protocol-endpoint observables $\widehat{K}, \mathcal{F}$ produced by running full-SCC gradient flow under some specific protocol. Empirically multi-formation under broad conditions.

For nearly a year, papers and notes had been comparing these two quantities as if they had to agree, and treating their disagreement as a paradox. **CN15 simply makes explicit that they refer to different things on different layers.** Once that's stated, the "paradox" dissolves — there's no rule saying static minima have to equal dynamic endpoints, and demanding such agreement was always an unstated premise rather than a derived requirement.

This pattern — *the apparent open problem dissolves once you state explicitly an assumption that had been operating implicitly* — is the kind of thing that's hard to get to by direct attack, and is why Option D wasn't on the candidate list at the start of the week.

### Introducing the σ-framework

The Hessian eigenvalue / irreducible-representation / nodal-count signature
$$\sigma(u^*) = (\mathcal{F}; \{(n_k, [\rho_k], \lambda_k)\})$$
classifies single-formation critical points by structural invariants that are robust to perturbation: the formation count $\mathcal{F}$, the irreducible-representation type $[\rho_k]$ of each Hessian-eigenspace, the number of nodes $n_k$, and the eigenvalues $\lambda_k$. The classification is well-posed on the smooth single-formation manifold $\Sigma_m$ (no corners) and serves three roles in W4:

1. **It sidesteps MO-1.** Single-formation σ doesn't require Morse on $\Sigma^K_M$, so the corner structure of multi-formation manifolds isn't a blocker for the W4 results.
2. **It provides a language for distinguishing critical points** that share gross features (same $\mathcal{F}$, same eigenvalues) but differ in fine structure (irrep type, nodal count). T-V5b-T's universal nodal-count = 2 result is a σ-statement about the Goldstone subspace.
3. **It's a candidate for extension** — multi-formation Phase 5 (W5+) would lift σ from $\Sigma_m$ to $\Sigma^K_M$, where it naturally couples to stratified Morse theory.

### The value of a verification reflex

V5b reaching V5b-T (Cat A) only after eight iterations — and the NQ-172 mode-indexing artifact being identified along the way — is evidence that the verification process is healthy. Premature promotion at the first iteration (or the third, or even the fifth) would have risked introducing a spurious result into canonical. The eight-iteration cost was visible; the cost of *not* doing those iterations would have been invisible until much later. That kind of asymmetric cost is exactly what verification regimes are designed to catch.

The general principle: the rate at which theorems are added to canonical is bounded *not* by how fast new derivations can be done, but by how fast verification can be completed without shortcuts. W4 spent a quarter of its days on verification (days 7 and 8). That ratio is sustainable and worth keeping.
